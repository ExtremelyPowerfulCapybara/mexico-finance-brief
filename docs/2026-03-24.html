Oil falls more than 13% after Trump's pause on Iran threats, but Tehran denies any contact with Washington
Trump announced on Truth Social that he would delay any attack on Iranian power plants for five days following 'very positive conversations,' sending WTI down 7.52% to US$90.84 and Brent down 6.55% to US$104.84, while European equities and Wall Street futures staged a sharp rebound. The problem: minutes later, two semi-official Iranian news agencies denied any communication with Washington, turning the pause into an act of faith. The conflict, which began on February 28 with U.S. and Israeli strikes against Iran, has reduced traffic through the Strait of Hormuz — a chokepoint for 20% of global oil and gas flows — to near zero, and has forced Iraq and Kuwait to shut in wells due to storage saturation.

TMEC 2.0: el Congreso de EE.UU., la influencia china y los minerales críticos, los tres ejes que definirán el nuevo tratado
Con las negociaciones entre México y Estados Unidos en marcha, el principal nudo es si el Congreso estadounidense deberá ratificar los acuerdos alcanzados — algo que México quiere evitar para no repetir los cambios de última hora que históricamente han desnaturalizado los textos negociados. El tercer eje es la negociación separada de minerales críticos — que Washington impulsa y Canadá rechaza por ser su principal carta de presión — en un tratado que, en la práctica, tendrá mucho menos de trilateral que su predecesor.

USMCA 2.0: Congressional ratification, Chinese influence, and critical minerals are the three axes that will define the new treaty
With negotiations between Mexico and the United States underway, the central knot is whether the U.S. Congress will need to ratify whatever is agreed — something Mexico wants to avoid in order not to repeat the last-minute changes that have historically distorted negotiated texts. Trump also wants to bypass Congress, partly because he anticipates losing the House majority in January, though any deal lacking ratification will carry weaker institutional staying power. The third axis is the separate negotiation of critical minerals — which Washington is pushing and Canada rejects as one of its main leverage cards — in a treaty that will in practice be far less trilateral than its predecessor.

At least 15 Argentine provinces face teacher salary conflicts as their fiscal position deteriorates
Argentine governors are privately admitting their books no longer balance: local revenue is weak, federal transfers have fallen, and the fiscal surpluses most provinces accumulated through 2024 appear to have been wiped out. Teachers are demanding wage increases that governors say they cannot afford, and the word 'shield' has begun circulating in conversations with the Casa Rosada as a euphemism for asking the federal government to cede funds — something Finance Minister Caputo, who held a 0.4% of GDP primary surplus in the first two months of the year, has no incentive to grant. The official bet is that the first installment of corporate income tax arriving in May will provide temporary relief, though one provincial economy minister put it plainly: 'That's not enough — without a real recovery, we're back to the same shortfalls.'
